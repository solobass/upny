# The UPNY Story

---

## The Room

Every year, across New York State, something remarkable happens in university conference rooms and competition auditoriums. Founders — many of them graduate students, researchers, or first-time entrepreneurs — stand up and present solutions to genuinely hard problems. Quantum sensors that could transform how we detect disease. Energy systems that could change how we power buildings. Materials science that could reshape manufacturing. Robotics that could make dangerous jobs safer.

These aren't napkin ideas. They've been incubated inside research institutions. The science has been stress-tested. The problems being solved are real.

And then the feedback comes.

*"Not VC ready. No customers. No revenue."*

Allan Von Schenkel heard this verdict repeated — at NYSBP competitions, at pitch events, at incubator demos across Upstate New York. The same founders. The same brilliant work. The same dismissal.

He kept waiting for someone to do something about it.

Eventually he realized: nobody was.

---

## The Real Problem

The standard interpretation of "not VC ready" is that the company is too early. But that's not quite right.

The companies weren't too early. The *founders* were undertrained — specifically on the one skill that separates a research project from a company: the ability to find and win customers.

Deep tech founders are builders by nature and training. They are physicists, engineers, materials scientists. They have spent years mastering the discipline of making things work. Nobody taught them how to sell. Nobody walked them through identifying an ideal customer, building a pipeline, testing a value proposition, or converting early interest into a signed agreement.

The bottleneck wasn't the science. It was the go-to-market.

And no one in the Upstate NY ecosystem was filling that gap.

---

## The Gap

University incubators do their job well: they produce founders and validate technology. Traditional venture capital does its job well — at Series A and beyond, once traction is proven.

The space between them — pre-seed, post-incubation, pre-revenue — is largely empty in Upstate New York.

There's no shortage of deep tech potential here. SUNY, Cornell, RPI, University at Albany, Clarkson, and others produce world-class research and a steady pipeline of founders ready to commercialize. The state has invested heavily in building that upstream capacity.

What happens next is the problem. Without pre-seed capital and hands-on GTM support, founders stall. Some give up. Some leave New York for ecosystems that have the infrastructure. The technology that New York taxpayers helped fund never reaches the market it was meant to serve.

---

## What UPNY Does

UPNY Ventures is a pre-seed venture fund built specifically for this gap.

We back university-incubated deep tech founders — in quantum, energy, advanced materials, robotics, biotech, and other hard technology domains — with two things they don't have and can't easily get anywhere else:

**Capital.** Pre-seed checks that don't require revenue or customers — just a credible founder, a real problem, and a fundable path to growth.

**GTM support.** A structured, hands-on commercialization program that teaches founders how to find customers, build pipeline, test their value proposition, and achieve the early validation milestones that unlock the next round of funding.

The program runs in four phases: a free 2-week GTM Strategy Course open to all founders; the 100-Day Challenge, a hands-on commercialization engine; a $25–50K pre-seed investment for top performers; and a $100K follow-on investment for companies that demonstrate continued traction.

The goal isn't to turn scientists into salespeople. It's to give them the tools, the process, and the support to get their first customers — so that the next time they walk into a room, the verdict isn't "not VC ready."

---

## Why Now

Deep tech is having a moment. Quantum computing, advanced energy systems, robotics, and materials science are moving from laboratory curiosity to commercial relevance. The window to back the foundational companies in these categories — before the category is defined, before the obvious winners emerge — is open now.

Upstate New York is positioned to be a meaningful part of this story. The research infrastructure is here. The talent is here. The problems being solved are real and large.

What's been missing is the commercialization layer. UPNY exists to build it.

## The Founder

Allan Von Schenkel has spent twenty years scaling software companies — as a five-time founder, as an operator, and as someone who has built and run accelerator programs inside venture-backed firms.

He didn't come to deep tech from the outside. He grew up off-grid, with wind turbines on the property. He has spent years mentoring quantum and energy founders coming out of New York's university system. He has sat in those pitch competition rooms and watched the same scene play out too many times.

UPNY is built from that experience — from knowing what these founders are missing, and from believing that the gap between the lab and the market is a solvable problem.
